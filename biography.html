Biography

James Kajdasz, Ph.D. — 20+ years across military intelligence, behavioral science research, and applied data science

The Air Force Academy and Early Assignments

I earned a B.S. in Behavioral Sciences & Leadership from the United States Air Force Academy and was commissioned as a Second Lieutenant. My first assignment was as a Public Affairs Staff Officer at USAFA's Barry Goldwater Visitor Center, the most-visited man-made tourist site in Colorado, where I redesigned exhibits, escorted VIP guests, and helped modernize the center's displays for the 850,000 visitors it served each year.

Finding a Home in Intelligence

I began aviation training at NAS Pensacola before deciding the intelligence career field was a better fit, and went on to graduate the Air Force's Intelligence Officer Course at Goodfellow AFB as an Outstanding Student with a 98 percent academic average. That course set the direction for the next two decades of my career.

Special Operations Intelligence Support

From 1999 to 2002 I supported the 720th Special Tactics Group at Hurlburt Field, Florida, providing ground intelligence to combat control and pararescue teams. I helped build the Special Tactics Automated Geographic Intelligence Suite by integrating the Air Force's FalconView software with the Army's TerraBase mapping system, giving mission planners a shared way to visualize terrain, threats, and routes before every deployment. I was promoted to Captain during this tour.

Counterterrorism After September 11th

In 2001 I deployed to Prince Sultan Air Base in the Kingdom of Saudi Arabia as Officer in Charge of the Counterintelligence Analysis Cell, supporting Operations Southern Watch and Enduring Freedom in the immediate aftermath of the September 11th attacks. I built a theater-wide terrorist-threat reporting system used by Joint Task Force-Southwest Asia to track and disseminate threat intelligence across the region, and the cell was recognized with AFOSI Region 2's "Team of the Year" award.

Graduate School and the Classroom

I was competitively selected for an Air Force-sponsored graduate program and earned an M.A. in Industrial & Organizational Psychology from George Mason University in preparation for a faculty position at the Air Force Academy. From 2003 to 2006 I served as an instructor and department budget officer in USAFA's Department of Behavioral Sciences & Leadership, teaching cadets, managing the department's budget, and mentoring cadets through the Academy's leadership development programs.

Intelligence Operations in Korea

From 2006 to 2007 I served with the 607th Air Intelligence Squadron at Osan Air Base, Republic of Korea, first as Chief of ISR Plans & Programs and then as Commander of the ISR Operations Flight. As Senior Intelligence Duty Officer, I directed intelligence, surveillance, and reconnaissance operations during the heightened tensions surrounding North Korea's 2006 nuclear test, integrating national and theater collection assets in support of Seventh Air Force.

Doctoral Research at Ohio State

Selected for a full-time doctoral sponsorship, I earned a Ph.D. in Quantitative Psychology, focused on judgment and decision making, from The Ohio State University. My dissertation, "Face Validity and Decision Aid Neglect," examined how the perceived credibility of a decision aid shapes whether the person using it actually trusts and adopts it. I presented this research at the Summer Institute on Bounded Rationality at the Max Planck Institute for Human Development in Berlin, and received the William James Award for Best Poster at Ohio State.

Leading Intelligence Education

From 2010 to 2014 I served on the faculty of the National Intelligence University, teaching graduate and undergraduate courses to more than 700 military and civilian intelligence professionals from DIA, NSA, and NGA. I chaired NIU's Institutional Review Board, reviewing over 400 research proposals, represented NIU on IARPA funding committees, and published research in the CIA's flagship journal, "Studies in Intelligence." I was promoted to Lieutenant Colonel during this period and went on to serve as the Air Force Senior Service Advisor to the University.

Research Leadership and Cyber

I returned to the Air Force Academy in 2014, first as an Assistant Professor in the Department of Behavioral Sciences & Leadership and then as Deputy Director of the Institute for Information Technology Applications, serving as the Air Force Research Laboratory's liaison to USAFA's multimillion-dollar research enterprise. I led research programs in remotely piloted aircraft command-and-control and cyber operations, and was recruited to a three-year NATO panel focused on communicating risk and uncertainty to policymakers.

Countering ISIS Information Operations

In 2017 I deployed to Al Udeid Air Base, Qatar, serving as J3 Behavioral Scientist with the Military Information Support Task Force-Central. I led operations-assessment efforts for U.S. information campaigns across roughly nine countries aimed at reducing support for ISIS, designing psychometric measurement frameworks and baseline assessments used to track the effectiveness of Military Information Support Operations over time.

Transition to Data Science

After more than two decades of service, I retired from the Air Force at the rank of Lieutenant Colonel. I then earned a Master of Information and Data Science from UC Berkeley, translating my background in intelligence analysis and behavioral science into modern machine learning and applied statistics. I went on to complete the Insight Data Science Fellows Program, a competitive, 15-week data science fellowship in Los Angeles, sharpening my applied machine learning skills alongside other data scientists transitioning into industry.

Machine Learning and Fraud Analytics

From 2020 to 2022 I worked as a Machine Learning Engineer at Electromagnetic Systems, Inc., building CNN-based automatic target recognition models on government sensor imagery within AWS GovCloud. Since 2022 I've been a Senior Data Scientist at Thomson Reuters Special Services, leading fraud analytics strategy for a California public-benefits fraud engagement spanning unemployment insurance, disability insurance, and hospice fraud — work that has contributed to identifying more than $1 billion in fraudulent claims exposure and generated over 100 high-value investigative leads tied to synthetic identity fraud, mule networks, and coordinated fraud rings.
